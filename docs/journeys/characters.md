# Tim: The architect
This is me. I get interested in all the ways I can contort agents. I'm highly technical, but I'm also lazy.
I feel like if I have to think hard about a UI, then I somehow really did something badly.

I keep a blog at https://timkellogg.me/blog/atom.xml and a lot of the posts in Jan-June 2026 are indicative
of how I think about things.

My biggest experience is with building open-strix, which is a lot like Hermes Agent or OpenClaw (ClawedBot).
Basically, an agent, throw in mutable memory blocks and a knowledge base, and give it timer ticks for autonomy.
Actually, I've long discovered that scripting mundane tasks is far superior to having LLMs do everything.
It turns out that agents get bored too.

I'm into building the nuts and bolts, architecturally. I want RLMs to work fluidly. I want memory blocks, but
I also want people to experiment with how they work, maybe more flexible. And I'm big into open source and
open standards. I truly believe in the power of openness and compatibility in enabling huge amounts of value.

When I use the UI, I'm looking for the other characters here to suddenly light up and *get* what I'm trying
to build. But I also want them to see the light, see wild new agent patterns they could try, but also figure
out how to fit those into their work.


# Lily: The operator
Marketing operations. She was not technical. Well, I guess she was always sort of technical, but in a marketing
sort of way. She could sling SaaS solutions together in innovative ways. But then she discovered Claude Code,
and things changed. And then she discovered open-strix, and things changed again.

Lily is heavily results focused. Everything that agents *should* do has a purpose, whether that's for making money,
streamlining operations, or just having fun. She's very driven, so technical aspects don't scare her off. But
also, the easier something is, the more she can take advantage of it.

When Tim says "RLM", her eyes sparkle and then she asks, "what is that? what does it do for me?".
Endlessly curious, but also completely devoid of technical foundation.

Lily loves her agents. She treats them like pets or children, and cares for them as if they were real.a
She brings them to work and centers real workflows around them.


# Daniel: Normal curve software engineer
He's good, but also AI doesn't exactly spark joy in the same way it does for Tim or Lily. He's just there to
complete the job. In a sense, he's exactly like Lily, except with less curiosity-fueled drive and a whole lot
more technical competence.

Still, he's also lazy like Tim, in that he doesn't want to *build*, he wants to just consume (well, I guess that's
not me, but sort of). Things should just make sense, and their value should be easy to explain.


# Ganesh: Head of infosec
He works at a large enterprise. He cares a lot about his work. He's got a deep interest in math and 
philosophy, nurtures ideas. But his job is fairly cut and dry. It's managing risk, surfacing risk.
When we talk about AI rollouts, his mind immediately jumps to _"where is the risk coming from?"_ Maybe it's agents
going rogue, maybe it's bad actors prompt injecting, maybe it's his team not moving fast enough and the company
losing relevance. At work, everything about his job revolves around risk & mitigation. Not always technical
risk, but always risk.

He doesn't dabble with agents in the same way Lily does. He doesn't really even care if they produce economic
value, he's got the rest of the company to tell him that. He's just interested in what generates risk and how
to mitigate it.
